What Are Stress and Anxiety?

Stress

Stress is your body's response to an external demand or threat. It is triggered by identifiable situations — a looming deadline, a difficult conversation, financial pressure, or a major life change. Stress is typically temporary: once the stressor is resolved, the tension fades. In small doses, stress can even be motivating.

Anxiety

Anxiety is a persistent feeling of worry or dread that continues even when there is no clear external trigger. It is internally driven and often disproportionate to the situation. While everyone experiences occasional anxiety, clinical anxiety disorders involve chronic worry that interferes with daily life and does not resolve on its own.

Shared and Unique Symptoms

Symptoms They Share

  • Increased heart rate and muscle tension
  • Difficulty sleeping or staying asleep
  • Trouble concentrating
  • Irritability and mood swings
  • Headaches and fatigue

Stress-Specific Symptoms

  • Feeling overwhelmed by a specific task or situation
  • Procrastination or avoidance of the stressor
  • Changes in appetite (eating more or less than usual)
  • Quick recovery once the situation is resolved

Anxiety-Specific Symptoms

  • Persistent worry without a clear cause
  • Feeling of impending doom or dread
  • Panic attacks (sudden episodes of intense fear)
  • Avoidance of non-dangerous situations
  • Physical symptoms like dizziness, nausea, or shortness of breath

When Does Each Occur?

Stress shows up when you face a specific challenge. Before a job interview, during exam season, after a conflict with a partner — these are all classic stress triggers. The key question is: can you point to the cause? If yes, it is probably stress.

Anxiety lingers even when things seem fine on the surface. You might have a great day at work, come home to a calm evening, and still feel a knot in your stomach. If your worry feels constant, vague, and difficult to control, anxiety may be at play.

It is also possible to experience both simultaneously. Chronic stress that goes unmanaged can evolve into generalized anxiety, making it feel impossible to separate the two. This is one reason why self-assessment tools like the Stress Check can be valuable — they help you establish a baseline.

Coping Strategies

For Stress

Since stress has an identifiable cause, the most effective approach is to address or remove the stressor. Break large tasks into smaller steps. Prioritize ruthlessly. Practice time management. Physical exercise, even a 10-minute walk, can dramatically reduce cortisol levels. Talk to someone you trust about what you are facing.

For Anxiety

Anxiety often requires a different toolkit. Mindfulness meditation and deep breathing exercises help calm the nervous system. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is one of the most effective treatments, helping you identify and reframe anxious thought patterns. Limiting caffeine and alcohol, maintaining a regular sleep schedule, and journaling your worries can also reduce symptoms. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the main difference between stress and anxiety?

Stress is a response to an external trigger and typically fades once the trigger is removed. Anxiety is persistent worry that continues even without an obvious external cause and is internally driven.

Can stress turn into anxiety?

Yes, chronic or unmanaged stress can develop into an anxiety disorder over time. When your nervous system stays on high alert for extended periods, it can become conditioned to remain anxious even after stressors are resolved.

How do I know if I have anxiety or just stress?

Ask yourself: can I identify a specific cause? If yes, it is likely stress. Does the worry persist even when things are going well? If yes, it may be anxiety. Persistent worry across multiple areas of life without clear triggers points toward anxiety.

What are the physical symptoms of stress vs anxiety?

Both share symptoms like increased heart rate, tension, and sleep disruption. Stress often causes fatigue and irritability tied to a known cause. Anxiety may additionally include shortness of breath, dizziness, and a persistent sense of dread without a clear trigger.

When should I seek professional help?

Seek help if your stress or anxiety interferes with daily functioning, lasts more than two weeks, causes avoidance of normal activities, disrupts sleep most nights, or leads to physical symptoms like chest pain or panic attacks.
